Instruments

Listen. Measure. Know before you pay.

The instruments below carry this garage's full diagnostic knowledge: fault trees, acoustic signatures, fluid charts, fair-shop playbooks. Plug in a FREE Gemini key (aistudio.google.com/apikey, any Google account, no card) and a frontier AI reasons over that knowledge, reads your photos, and writes your diagnosis live. Without a key everything still works: the on-device Advisor model narrates, and photos use the local color analyzer.

Your car is talking. Record the clunk, squeal, tick, or hiss, or photograph the leak, and watch the AI reason over real spectral evidence to tell you what is wrong, how urgent it is, and exactly what to say at the counter so nobody overcharges you.

About this instrument

Every fault has a voiceprint. A worn belt screams one narrow note; a dying rod bearing thuds at half engine speed; a boost leak hisses with no rhythm at all. This instrument runs your recording through the same math a vibration lab uses: windowed frequency analysis plus an envelope-rhythm scan, measuring the pitch, purity, and beat of the noise. Those measurements are matched against a built-in library of acoustic fault signatures with full playbooks: causes, urgency, fair-shop pricing behavior, free driveway checks, and the follow-up questions that separate look-alike sounds. Then the AI engine composes your diagnosis from that evidence, live, token by token. It is a hypothesis, not a verdict, and it says so: but you will walk into the shop knowing exactly what to ask for. Photos work the same way: fluid-color charts and wear-pattern guides feed the vision model, so a picture of the puddle under your car comes back as a name, a severity, and a plan.

Plan the modification before you spend a dollar. Slide the power up, strip the weight, pick the tires: physics-true performance deltas, a half-shaft safety factor at launch torque, clutch heat per launch, and a live finite-element stress map that warns you BEFORE a mod snaps your stock axles.

About this instrument

F = ma is not a slogan here; it is the solver. Your planned build runs through the same physics engine that powers the whole garage: traction-limited launch, power-limited pull, drag and downforce, so the 0 to 60, top speed, and braking numbers are derived, not guessed. Then the driveline check: peak launch torque is computed from tire grip and weight, pushed through a half-shaft shear-stress formula, and compared with your chosen material's yield to produce a safety factor. Below one, the axle fails: the app tells you before the parking lot does. The colored map at the bottom is a genuine finite element analysis, the same method engineering firms use: the torque-reaction bracket is meshed into elements, a stiffness matrix is assembled and solved right here in your browser, and the von Mises stress field paints cool teal to danger ruby. Drag the power slider and watch the metal feel it.

Weigh your cargo with a door slam. Loading for a move, a lumber run, a packed road trip? Calibrate empty, load up, slam the door once, and the app tells you how many kilograms you added and how close the suspension is to its rated payload: before the highway finds out for you.

About this instrument

Your car sits on springs, and any mass on springs has a natural frequency: the rate it bobs when disturbed. Add cargo and that frequency drops, by exactly the square-root law engineers write as omega equals root k over m. A door slam is a free impulse hammer: the chassis wobbles for a second, your phone's accelerometer records the wobble, and the app measures its frequency with a self-calibrating estimator that cancels its own bias. Compare loaded against your empty calibration and the added mass falls out of the algebra: no truck scale, no hardware, just physics. Real use needs the phone flat on a hard interior surface with the engine off, and honest tolerance is fifteen to twenty-five percent: this is a load-awareness instrument, not a certified scale. The Simulate buttons inject a synthetic slam through the identical pipeline and recover a known 320 kg to within a few kilograms: the proof that the math is real.

Know your brakes before the shop tells you. Hold the phone near a front wheel, tap the brakes while rolling slowly, and the instrument listens for the wear indicator's signature: your leverage against both the upseller and the expensive sound of metal on metal.

About this instrument

Brake pads ship with a small steel tab tuned to sing between roughly 1.8 and 4.5 kilohertz when the friction material wears down to replacement depth: it is a designed-in alarm. This instrument isolates exactly that band against the rest of the spectrum and returns one of three states. Quiet band: pads healthy, decline the upsell with confidence. Clean tone in the band: the indicator is touching, roughly the two to three millimeter point, plan a replacement soon. Broadband grinding with no clean tone: friction material gone, every stop is machining your rotors, park it. One honest limit, stated plainly: millimeter-precise thickness cannot be measured acoustically by any phone: the tab is a threshold announcer, and this instrument reports the truth the tab was built to tell.
